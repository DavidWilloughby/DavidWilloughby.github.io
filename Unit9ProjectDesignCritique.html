Step 2: Reviews

Medium (http://medium.com)

I was a regular reader of Medium when I first bought a smartphone and this was in part because it does a very good job of enabling long-form reading on a small screen. It achieves this through generous apportioning of white space along with well-selected typefaces and font stylings (heavyweight sans-serif for titles, a serif font with large x-height for reading, and light grey sans-serif for almost everything else).

There are prompts to explore other content but these don’t compete too much for your attention. In-text links are also kept to an absolute minimum. The full-screen layout has a sticky right sidebar which includes the latest trending articles, but this is only visible on the front page and not on individual articles.

The overall effect is one of reading a fairly highbrow glossy magazine. Design choices that Medium pioneered (or at least popularised) have since been adopted by many other online publishers, marking a shift away from hyperlinks and metacontent in a lot of blogging and other magazine-type content online. This is a good example of how web design can be given a reboot by borrowing dormant tropes from old print media, which in recent years has meant more white space, pull quotes, and full-width photographs appearing online.

My main objection to the Medium website as it looks now is that they’ve allowed the front page to become a bit of a mess, hierarchy-wise. By pulling only the first sentence from each article, the site makes it difficult to judge a writer by style or technique (or even subject matter) and places more emphasis on the use of an arresting image or clickable headline to attract readers. This seems to be flying in the opposite direction from the site’s stated goal of publishing high-quality writing. Medium should adopt a more curatorial and editorial approach on its front page by leading with full-paragraph quotes from the best writers in a header section and perhaps breaking down the remainder of its content into more clearly defined editorial sections for ease of navigation.

Mimar Architects (http://mimarch.net)

I was certain this had to be a spoof website but apparent ongoing efforts to promote the company’s business suggest that it may actually be legitimate. It would take too long to list all the horrors, but special mention should be given to the lack of a recognisable colour scheme or focal point, the arbitrary use of uppercase for navigation rather than text color or weight, and comically awful staff portraits better suited to an offline idiom such as an early 1990s corporate brochure. At least the site is consistent with the company’s design products, which appear to be universally badly conceived.
